# Exercise 01 - Getting to know your customer

## What this exercise is testing

This exercise is testing whether you can walk into a discovery meeting prepared. A solution architect is expected to understand the customer's business context before discussing technology. The goal is not to collect random facts. The goal is to gather information that changes how you will lead conversations, identify risk, and shape a proposal.

## How to analyze it

Use four lenses.

### 1. Business and operating model

Find out how the company makes money, which divisions matter most, and whether it is centralized or highly regional. This affects process standardization, ownership, reporting, and deployment complexity.

### 2. Stakeholders and decision makers

Identify likely executive sponsors, operational leaders, and technical influencers. You are not just collecting names. You are mapping who owns budget, who owns the problem, and who can block design decisions.

### 3. Current pressures

Review earnings calls, press releases, analyst commentary, and public complaints. You are looking for pain points such as customer churn, service delays, acquisitions, cost pressure, compliance exposure, or product quality issues.

### 4. Technology posture

Look for cloud preference, Microsoft footprint, merger history, security sensitivity, and public technology partnerships. This tells you whether the organization is likely to prefer first-party capabilities, conservative governance, or a more integration-heavy approach.

## Recommended approach

Prepare a short customer briefing with these sections:

1. Company summary: industry, revenue profile, geography, major business units.
2. Executive map: three executives, their likely relevance, and what each probably cares about.
3. Business pressures: three to five recent signals that may shape priorities.
4. Technology indicators: Microsoft usage, cloud preferences, digital maturity, acquisition complexity.
5. Meeting strategy: questions you should ask first because the research suggests they matter.

## What good output looks like

Do not stop at facts such as stock symbol or headquarters. Turn facts into implications.

Examples:

- If the company has many regions, highlight possible localization, data residency, and security model complexity.
- If press releases emphasize customer experience, expect investment in self-service, case management, and analytics.
- If the company recently acquired another business, expect data quality issues, duplicate systems, and change management challenges.

## Architect answer pattern

When presenting your findings, explain:

- what you found
- why it matters to the solution
- which assumptions still need to be validated in discovery

## Risks to call out

- Public information can be incomplete or outdated.
- Executive names do not prove project sponsorship.
- Technology clues do not replace direct confirmation of current architecture.

## A strong solution-architect response

"I would research the customer using executive, financial, operational, and technology sources. I would not just compile facts; I would convert them into solution implications. For example, a geographically distributed organization suggests business unit, security, performance, and support-model decisions. Recent customer-service pain in the news suggests prioritizing service operations, self-service, analytics, and escalation management in discovery. I would bring a short briefing and a question list to validate assumptions in the first meeting."
